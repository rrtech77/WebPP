What does Theravada say about non duality?


In Theravada Buddhism, the concept of non-duality (advaya in Sanskrit) isn't a central or explicit teaching in the same way it is in some other Buddhist traditions, like Mahayana (particularly in schools like Zen and Dzogchen). However, there are interpretations of Theravada teachings that touch upon ideas related to non-duality, though the framework and language may differ.

Key Concepts in Theravada and How They Relate to Non-Duality

  1. Anatta (Non-Self): Theravada emphasizes the doctrine of anatta, which means "not-self" or "non-self." This teaching states that there is no permanent, unchanging soul or self. All phenomena, including the self, are seen as impermanent (anicca), unsatisfactory (dukkha), and without an inherent self (anatta). This could be seen as a form of non-duality, in that there is no separation between a permanent self and the rest of existence. Everything is interdependent and part of a continuous process.

  2. Emptiness (Sunyata): While the concept of Sunyata (emptiness) is more heavily explored in Mahayana schools, Theravada also acknowledges it. Emptiness refers to the idea that all things are empty of a permanent, independent essence. In a way, this reflects non-dualism, as no phenomenon exists in isolation from the rest of reality.

  3. Dependent Origination (Paticcasamuppada): This foundational teaching in Theravada explains how all phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions. It demonstrates the interconnectedness and conditionality of all experiences. While it doesn't directly use the language of non-duality, this interdependent view can point toward a non-dual understanding of reality, where there is no inherent separation between things, only interrelated processes.

  4. Nibbana (Nirvana): In Theravada, the ultimate goal of the spiritual path is nibbana, which is often described as the cessation of suffering and the extinguishing of ignorance, craving, and attachment. Some interpretations of nibbana might align with non-duality, as it represents the dissolution of all distinctions and dualities, including self and other, subject and object.

Non-Duality in Theravada vs. Mahayana

Theravada does not emphasize non-duality in the same way as Mahayana schools like Madhyamaka or Yogacara, which explicitly explore the idea that all dualistic distinctions (subject-object, self-other) are ultimately illusory. Instead, Theravada focuses more on the practical path to liberation through ethical conduct, meditation, and insight into the nature of reality (impermanence, suffering, non-self).

While some scholars or practitioners may interpret aspects of Theravada in a non-dualistic light, the tradition itself tends to emphasize a gradual, structured path to liberation rather than an immediate recognition of non-dual awareness.

In short, while Theravada may not use the language of "non-duality," its core teachings on non-self, emptiness, and interdependence can be understood as pointing toward a reality that transcends dualistic distinctions.


What is Yogacara?


Yogacarya (Sanskrit for "Practice of Yoga") is one of the two major schools of Mahayana Buddhist philosophy, alongside Madhyamaka. It is also known as Vijnanavada or the "Consciousness-Only" school. Yogacara focuses primarily on the nature of consciousness and how reality is perceived. It explores how our experiences are shaped by the mind and seeks to explain the mechanisms of perception, cognition, and enlightenment.

Key Teachings of Yogacara

  1. Cittamatra (Mind-Only) or Vijnyaptimatra (Consciousness-Only): The core teaching of Yogacara is that all phenomena are products of the mind. In other words, everything we experience is a mental construct, and there is no independent, external world existing apart from consciousness. This is not to say that nothing exists, but rather that our perceptions are filtered through and shaped by mental processes. Reality as we experience it is a projection of consciousness.

  2. The Three Natures (Trisvabhava): Yogacara explains reality through the concept of three natures:

    • Parikalpita-svabhava (Imagined Nature): This refers to the illusion of duality and separation, where we mistakenly perceive things as existing independently and inherently.
    • Paratantra-svabhava (Dependent Nature): This refers to the understanding that all phenomena arise dependently based on causes and conditions, much like the concept of dependent origination (paticcasamuppada) in Theravada.
    • Parini·π£panna-svabhava (Perfected Nature): This refers to the ultimate, non-dual nature of reality, where one recognizes that all things are empty of inherent existence and are mind-only constructs.

  3. Alaya-vijnana (Storehouse Consciousness): One of Yogacara's most distinctive teachings is the concept of alaya-vijnana, often translated as "storehouse consciousness." This is a deep layer of consciousness that contains the karmic seeds (or impressions) of past actions and experiences. These seeds mature into future experiences, shaping how we perceive the world. It serves as the foundation for individual experience and continuity across lifetimes, even though it is ultimately impermanent and subject to transformation.

  4. Eight Consciousnesses: Yogacara breaks down human cognition into eight types of consciousness:

    • The first five correspond to the senses: sight, hearing, taste, touch, and smell.
    • The sixth is the manovijnyana, or mental consciousness, which processes sensory input and conceptual thinking.
    • The seventh is afflicted mind, which clings to the idea of an inherent self.
    • The eighth is the storehouse consciousness, where karmic imprints are stored.

  5. Non-Duality: Yogacara emphasizes non-duality as an essential realization on the path to enlightenment. Dualistic thinking such as the division between subject and object, self and other is seen as a fundamental delusion. Through deep insight and meditation, one can realize the non-dual nature of consciousness, where such distinctions dissolve, and reality is understood as a seamless, interconnected whole.

  6. Transformation of Consciousness: Enlightenment in Yogacara is described as the transformation of consciousness. This transformation involves the eradication of ignorance and the false perception of duality, leading to the realization of the true nature of mind and reality. The alaya-vijnyana is transformed into wisdom, and the afflicted mind  gives way to non-dual awareness.

Comparison with Madhyamaka

  • Madhyamaka emphasizes emptiness (sunyata), arguing that all phenomena lack inherent existence, even consciousness itself. It deconstructs all conceptual frameworks, including those of Yogacara.
  • Yogacara, on the other hand, focuses on the nature of consciousness and how it shapes experience. While it also teaches the emptiness of external phenomena, it uses the model of mind-only to explore how reality is constructed through mental processes.

Yogacara and Practice

The name Yogacara suggests a close connection to meditative practice (yoga), and the school is deeply interested in how insights into the nature of consciousness can be cultivated through meditation. By understanding the workings of the mind and the way it creates dualistic delusions, practitioners can achieve a transformation in their experience of reality, ultimately leading to liberation.

Influence of Yogacara

Yogacara has had a profound influence on later Mahayana traditions, particularly in East Asia, where it contributed to the development of schools like Zen (Chan) in China and Japan, Huayan in China, and Tendai in Japan. Its concepts of mind-only and the storehouse consciousness also laid the groundwork for ideas in Tibetan Buddhism, especially within the Dzogchen and Mahamudra traditions.

In summary, Yogacara is a philosophical and meditative approach that emphasizes the central role of consciousness in shaping our experience of reality, offering a path to enlightenment through understanding and transforming the mind.


What is the difference between mind and non duality?


The difference between mind and non-duality can be understood through the distinct ways these concepts are approached in various philosophical and spiritual traditions, especially in Buddhism and non-dualistic schools of thought.

1. Mind (Citta or Vijnyana)

In most spiritual traditions, the mind is understood as the faculty of awareness, perception, thought, and consciousness. It is the seat of cognition, responsible for understanding, interpreting, and interacting with reality. In Buddhism, the mind is often discussed in relation to how it creates experiences, suffers, and can be purified for enlightenment.

  • In Buddhism, particularly Yogacara (Mind-Only school), the mind is seen as the creator of our experience of reality. All perceptions and phenomena are ultimately mental constructs, arising from the storehouse consciousness (alaya-vijnyana) and shaped by karma. The mind's processes, attachments, and defilements are what bind beings to samsara (the cycle of suffering), and purifying the mind is essential for liberation.

  • Mind is typically considered dualistic in its everyday function. It divides the world into subject and object, perceiver and perceived. The mind operates based on distinctions and categorizations, and much of the suffering comes from the minds attachment to these dualities.

  • In Western psychology, mind refers to thoughts, emotions, memory, and other cognitive functions. The mind is the tool through which we make sense of the world, but it is prone to illusions and misperceptions.

2. Non-Duality

Non-duality refers to the absence of dualism, meaning the dissolution of distinctions such as self/other, subject/object, or mind/world. In non-dualistic traditions, the ultimate reality is seen as one, unified, and indivisible. The dualistic thinking of the mind is seen as illusory or a product of ignorance.

  • In Buddhism (particularly Mahayana and schools like Zen, Dzogchen, or Mahamudra), non-duality is a realization that transcends the ordinary workings of the mind. It is an understanding that all distinctions are conceptual and that, at the deepest level, reality is interconnected, empty of inherent existence, and non-separate. In this state, the subject-object division dissolves, and one experiences the non-dual nature of reality.

  • In Advaita Vedanta (a non-dualistic Hindu philosophy), non-duality (Advaita) refers to the idea that the ultimate reality is Brahman, and the apparent distinctions we experience in the world, including the sense of self as separate from others or from the universe, are illusions (Maya). The true self (Atman) is not different from Brahman, and realizing this is the goal of spiritual practice.

The Relationship Between Mind and Non-Duality

  • Mind is Dualistic by Nature: The mind, as it functions in ordinary experience, perceives reality in terms of opposites or dualities (e.g., self vs. other, good vs. bad, subject vs. object). These dualities are conditioned by perception, mental habits, and karmic imprints, which make us experience the world as fragmented and divided.

  • Non-Duality is Beyond Mind: Non-dual awareness is the transcendence of the minds dualistic tendencies. In the non-dual state, the distinctions that the mind creates are recognized as illusory. Non-duality points to a reality that is beyond conceptual thought, beyond the subject-object split, where all phenomena are seen as interdependent and interconnected, without inherent separation.

Difference in Experience

  • Mind is the tool that shapes, interprets, and interacts with the world through cognition, but it can be clouded by delusion, attachment, and ignorance. When functioning in duality, the mind reinforces the sense of a separate self and distinct objects.

  • Non-duality represents an experiential realization that occurs when the minds habitual dualistic tendencies are seen through. In non-dual experience, there is no longer a subject that perceives objects as separate; instead, there is a direct, unmediated awareness of reality as one seamless whole.

Non-Duality and the Transformation of Mind

  • In Yogacarya Buddhism, the transformation of mind is necessary for realizing non-duality. The ordinary mind, which is bound by dualistic distinctions, is transformed into a state of pure wisdom, where the subject-object duality dissolves. This is often described as a shift from ignorance (avidya) to wisdom (prajnya).

  • In Dzogchen and Mahamudra, mind (rigpa) is seen as pure, primordial awareness, which is already non-dual in essence. The path is about recognizing this innate non-dual awareness that is always present but obscured by the mind's dualistic habits.

If an enlightened mind has realized non-duality, and embodies wisdom, compassion, and ethical behavior is that not a self?


This is an insightful question because it touches on the apparent paradox of how an enlightened being can embody qualities such as wisdom, compassion, and ethical behavior without being a "self" in the conventional sense. In Buddhism, particularly in the context of enlightenment and non-duality, the concept of self is radically redefined.

Key Points to Consider:

1. No-Self (Anatta) in Buddhism

  • One of the core teachings of Buddhism is the doctrine of anatta (Pali), or non-self. This doctrine states that there is no permanent, unchanging, or independent self or soul in any being or phenomenon. What we conventionally refer to as "self" is an illusion, a product of the five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness), which are in constant flux and dependent on causes and conditions.

  • Realizing anatta is central to the enlightened mind. Enlightenment means recognizing that the self we ordinarily identify with is a mental construct. There is no inherently existent, autonomous entity called "self" that operates independently from the rest of reality.

2. Non-Duality and Non-Self

  • In the state of non-duality, the dualistic distinctions such as self/other, subject/object dissolve. In this realization, the self is seen as just another conceptual distinction, not something inherently real.

  • An enlightened being operates without a sense of a separate, fixed self, but this does not mean they cease to function. Rather, they function without attachment to the concept of "I" or "mine." They engage with the world without the ego-driven motivations that are based on a sense of self.

3. Wisdom, Compassion, and Ethical Behavior

  • Wisdom (Prajnya): The wisdom of an enlightened being comes from seeing the true nature of reality--impermanence, non-self, and interdependence. This wisdom allows them to act skillfully, without attachment or delusion.

  • Compassion: Compassion arises naturally from the realization of non-duality. When there is no sense of a separate self, the suffering of others is not seen as separate from oneself. An enlightened being's compassion is selfless--it is not motivated by ego or self-interest, but by the understanding that all beings are interconnected and share the same nature.

  • Ethical Behavior: Ethical behavior flows from the realization of emptiness and non-duality. Ethical actions are not performed out of self-interest or the desire to accumulate merit for oneself. Instead, they arise from a spontaneous, selfless response to the needs of others and the recognition of the interconnectedness of all beings.

4. Is this a "Self"?

No, this is not a self in the conventional sense. When Buddhism speaks of an enlightened being embodying wisdom, compassion, and ethical behavior, it does not mean that there is a permanent, unchanging "self" behind these qualities.

  • The qualities of an enlightened being do not arise from a fixed, independent self. Instead, they arise spontaneously and naturally from the realization of non-self and non-duality. The enlightened being acts without clinging to the idea of "I" or "mine". Their actions are not based on ego or self-identity, but on the recognition of reality as it is--interconnected, impermanent, and empty of inherent self-nature.

5. Function Without Self

  • In Buddhism, an enlightened being can still function in the world--thinking, speaking, feeling, and acting--without a sense of an inherent self. Their actions are based on wisdom and compassion, not on self-referential thoughts. They do not identify with these actions as belonging to a self but perform them in alignment with the natural flow of dependent origination and non-duality.

Example in Buddhism: The Bodhisattva Ideal

In Mahayana Buddhism, the Bodhisattva is an enlightened being who works tirelessly for the benefit of all sentient beings, guided by wisdom and compassion. While a Bodhisattva engages in compassionate actions, they do so without a sense of self. In the Prajnyaparamita teachings (Perfection of Wisdom), it is said that a Bodhisattva understands that there is no inherent self in themselves or others, yet they continue to help all beings out of boundless compassion.

  • The Bodhisattva acts, but without ego. They do not cling to the idea of I am the one helping others because they have realized non-duality and non-self. Their actions are spontaneous, flowing from the recognition that all beings share the same empty nature.

Conclusion:

While an enlightened mind embodies qualities like wisdom, compassion, and ethical behavior, this does not imply the existence of a self in the way we ordinarily think of it. These qualities arise from the realization of non-duality and the recognition that the self is an illusion. Enlightened beings act without attachment to a fixed self, and their actions are not motivated by ego, but by the deep understanding of the interconnectedness of all things.

Thus, the embodiment of these qualities is not the expression of a self, but the natural, spontaneous activity of a mind that has realized the emptiness of self and the non-dual nature of reality.


Is the self just another conceptual distinction in the case that it is the observer of the mind, not the subject?


In the context of non-duality and many spiritual traditions, including Buddhism, Advaita Vedanta, and Yogic philosophies, the idea that the "self" is the observer rather than the subject of experience is still considered a conceptual distinction that arises from the mind. Even this notion of a separate observer is seen as part of the dualistic thinking that non-dual teachings aim to transcend.

1. The Observer as a Concept

When we speak of the self as the observer of the mind watching thoughts, emotions, and sensory experiences it might feel like there is a neutral, detached entity behind the flow of experiences. This is a common idea in many meditative traditions, where the practitioner is encouraged to observe the mind without identifying with its contents.

However, from the standpoint of non-duality and no-self (anatta), even this observer is part of the illusion of duality. The sense of being an observer creates a subtle division between the one who observes (subject) and that which is observed (object), reinforcing a sense of duality.

In the context of non-duality:

  • The observer itself is a mental construct. It is simply another layer of identification, a more refined version of the sense of self. While the observer may not be as gross or egoic as the personal self (the one identified with body, personality, etc.), it still carries a sense of separateness--I am the one watching.
  • Non-dual realization involves seeing through this final layer of identification, recognizing that even the observer is not ultimately separate from the observed. There is no independent entity that stands apart from the flow of experience.

2. Subject vs. Observer Distinction

In the context of your question, the distinction between subject and observer highlights different levels of identity:

  • Subject: In everyday experience, the subject is typically understood as the egoic self "I" who experiences and acts in the world. This is the self that feels, thinks, desires, and suffers.
  • Observer: In many meditative practices, the practitioner learns to step back from identifying with the egoic subject and becomes a detached observer of thoughts and feelings. The observer is often seen as a more subtle level of self-awareness, distinct from the usual sense of being a "doer."

However, from the standpoint of non-dual realization:

  • The observer is still dualistic. It remains a form of subtle identification, implying that there is a separate entity (the observer) apart from the mind and the world of experiences. This subtle division maintains a sense of separation.
  • True non-duality sees through both the subject and the observer. Ultimately, there is no inherent "self" to observe anything. The mind, body, and experiences arise spontaneously and interdependently, without needing a central "I" to observe them.

3. The Non-Dual Perspective: No Separate Observer

In the state of non-dual awareness, the distinction between observer and observed dissolves completely. Here are key points from non-dual teachings:

  • There is only the flow of experience: Instead of a separate observer watching the mind, there is simply awareness in which experiences (thoughts, sensations, perceptions) arise and pass away. No entity is standing outside of this process to observe it.

  • Awareness is not personal: In non-duality, awareness is often described as impersonal, meaning it is not owned or possessed by an individual self. There is no "someone" who is aware; there is just awareness itself. This awareness is not separate from what arises within it--it is a unified field of consciousness.

  • No central "I": The feeling that "I" am the observer is seen as a construction of the mind, a subtle form of ego. When this subtle identification is dropped, what remains is a non-dual awareness in which there is no observer, no observed, no subject, no object--just the unbroken, indivisible flow of experience.

4. Anatta (No-Self) in Buddhism

In Buddhism, particularly in the doctrine of anatta (non-self), the idea of a separate observer is also considered illusory. The Buddha taught that what we consider the self (whether as subject or observer) is a composite of impermanent and interdependent processes--the five aggregates (form, feeling, perception, mental formations, and consciousness).

  • There is no permanent observer: Even the observing consciousness is part of the flow of phenomena. It arises and passes away in response to conditions, just like thoughts, emotions, and perceptions. There is no unchanging "observer" that stands apart from the experience.
  • Liberation comes from seeing through all forms of identification: Both the egoic self (subject) and the subtle observer are seen as products of ignorance (avidya). Full enlightenment involves recognizing that these identifications are empty of inherent existence.

5. Advaita Vedanta and the Witness

In Advaita Vedanta, the concept of the witness (Sakshi) is sometimes invoked as the ultimate, unchanging observer of all experiences. However, even in Advaita, the final realization goes beyond the idea of the witness as a separate entity:

  • The witness (pure consciousness) is not an individual self. It is the universal, non-personal awareness in which all experiences arise.
  • Non-dual realization involves understanding that there is no separate entity observing anything. The witness and the witnessed are not-two (Advaita = "not two"). There is only Brahman, the undivided reality.

6. True Non-Dual Realization

In the ultimate non-dual realization, the distinction between observer and observed, self and other, subject and object collapses entirely. This means:

  • There is no separate self (whether as subject or observer) to be found.
  • What remains is pure awareness, which is non-dual--it is not divided into a watcher and something being watched. There is only the seamless, interconnected flow of experience without any center or reference point.

Conclusion:

Even if the self is understood as the observer of the mind, rather than the subject, this is still considered a conceptual distinction in the context of non-duality. The idea of an observer implies a subtle separation between the one observing and what is observed, which perpetuates dualistic thinking. Non-duality transcends this distinction by showing that there is no separate observer--just the continuous flow of awareness in which both the observer and the observed are seen as conceptual constructs, not ultimate realities. Awareness itself is not owned by a self; it is impersonal, indivisible, and free from duality.


What if the observer is not identifying with what is being observed?


If the observer is not identifying with what is being observed, this represents a significant step toward detachment and clarity of awareness, but it is still important to examine whether this position fully transcends the dualistic framework. Even in this state of non-identification, the question remains whether there is still an implicit division between the observer and the observed, and whether this separation perpetuates the subtle sense of a self.

1. Non-Identification as a Practice

In many meditative traditions, including Buddhism, Advaita Vedanta, and Yogic practices, practitioners are encouraged to cultivate the ability to observe the contents of their minds thoughts, emotions, sensations  without identifying with them. This practice of non-identification allows for a more neutral and spacious relationship with experience.

  • Buddhism teaches mindfulness (sati), where one observes thoughts, feelings, and sensations with a sense of detachment, recognizing them as impermanent and not-self. This is part of the path to seeing through the illusion of self.

  • Advaita Vedanta and other non-dual traditions emphasize the idea of being the witness or awareness that remains uninvolved with the play of thoughts and experiences, resting in pure consciousness.

In these practices, non-identification is seen as a way to reduce suffering, as it breaks the habitual patterns of attachment to desires, fears, and ego-based concerns. By observing without identifying, one reduces the power that these thoughts and emotions have over the mind.

2. Non-Identification and Non-Duality

However, non-identification alone does not necessarily lead to full non-dual realization. The act of observing without identifying can still maintain a subtle duality between the observer and the observed. In non-dual teachings, the goal is to dissolve this duality entirely, realizing that even the distinction between observer and observed is illusory.

Why?

  • The observer-observed distinction: Even if the observer does not identify with the contents of consciousness, the very idea of an observer still implies that there is someone or something separate that is observing. This creates a subtle sense of separateness or duality, which in non-dual teachings is considered part of the illusion.

  • Non-duality goes beyond detachment. In true non-dual awareness, the distinction between the one who observes and what is observed collapses. There is no longer an independent observer separate from experience; instead, there is only the flow of awareness or pure consciousness where the boundary between subject and object, observer and observed, disappears.

3. The Next Step: Collapsing the Observer-Observed Distinction

Non-identification is a significant step toward liberation, but in non-dual realization, even the observer is seen as part of the illusion. The sense that "I am the observer of my experience" is itself another layer of self-identification, albeit a subtler one.

In non-duality, one realizes that:

  • The observer is not a separate entity. The act of observation and what is observed arise together in a single, unified field of awareness. There is no separation between the two.
